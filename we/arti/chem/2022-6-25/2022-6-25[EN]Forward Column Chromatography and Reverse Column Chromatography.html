Forward Column Chromatography and Reverse Column Chromatography

The original language of this article is Chinese and parts of it have been translated using DeepL

 Infrared spectroscopy is used to analyse the structure of organic compounds. Specifically, a special instrument is used to emit infrared light and allow it to pass through a sample of an organic compound, and then the degree to which different wavelengths of infrared light are absorbed is measured, resulting in an image called an infrared spectrum.

 Infrared spectroscopy is a method of analytical chemistry that can be used to analyse the structure of organic compounds. Specifically, it uses the fact that different functional groups in different organic compounds vibrate differently under different wavelengths of infrared light, resulting in different absorption peaks, to determine which functional groups are present in an organic compound and their relative positions. Generally speaking, the stronger the absorption peak, the more functional groups there are; the sharper the peak, the more homogeneous the functional groups; and the wider the peak, the more complex the functional groups. I used as a sample an organic acid called phenylacetic acid, which has a carboxyl group and a benzene ring, which can be analysed by infrared spectroscopy.

 To do the experiment, I first ground the phenylacetic acid to a powder using a mortar and pestle, then measured a quantity of phenylacetic acid powder using a measuring cylinder and placed it in a small dish. Next, I used an IR spectrometer sample holder to hold the small plate in place and then placed the sample holder in the scanning area of the IR spectrometer. I then use a computer to control the operating parameters of the infrared spectrometer, such as the scan range, scan speed, number of scans, etc. Then, I pressed the start button and let the IR spectrometer start scanning the sample. I waited for about five minutes before I saw an infrared spectrogram displayed on the computer screen.

 To observe the results, I first opened the infrared spectrogram displayed on the computer screen using a software for infrared spectrograms and then used a magnifying glass to look closely at the absorption of infrared light at different wavelengths. I found a very strong and sharp absorption peak at around 1700 cm-1, indicating a carboxyl group in the sample, and a very weak and broad absorption peak at around 3100 cm-1, indicating an aromatic ring in the sample. This proved that my sample was phenylacetic acid and was not contaminated or spoiled. I feel that this experiment will allow me to learn more about IR spectroscopy.
