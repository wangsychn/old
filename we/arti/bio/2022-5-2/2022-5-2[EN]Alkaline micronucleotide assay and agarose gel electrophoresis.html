Alkaline micronucleotide assay and agarose gel electrophoresis

The original language of this article is Chinese and parts of it have been translated using DeepL 

  Today I completed a very interesting experiment in which I extracted plasmid DNA from E. coli using the alkaline lysis method and cut it with restriction endonucleases and then analysed it by electrophoresis. Plasmid DNA is a small circular DNA molecule that can replicate itself within a cell and can carry specific genes such as antibiotic resistance genes or fluorescent genes. Restriction endonucleases are enzymes that recognise and cut specific DNA sequences, which can be used to analyse the structure and function of DNA. Electrophoretic analysis is a method that uses an electric field to cause DNA molecules to migrate according to size, and it can be used to look at fragments and lengths of DNA.

 

 The process of this experiment is actually quite simple, it is to follow the steps given by the teacher, but there are some things that need to be taken care of. For example, when adding the alkaline solution, turn the tube gently and do not stir too vigorously as this will damage the structure of the DNA. Also, when adding a solution that has been stored frozen, it is important to put it in the refrigerator as soon as possible, as this will allow the DNA to precipitate. Finally, when carrying out electrophoretic analysis, add the DNA solution to the gel bath carefully, without letting it overflow or run off into another bath.

 

 This experiment turned out pretty well as I managed to extract the plasmid DNA from E. coli and after cutting it with restriction endonuclease, I got the expected electrophoretic profile. From the electrophoretic profile, I could see that the uncut plasmid DNA only had a band of about 2000bp, while the cut plasmid DNA had two bands, one of about 2000bp and one of about 560bp. This shows that the restriction endonuclease I used was able to find two cut sites on the plasmid DNA and cut it into two fragments. These two fragments are the vector part of the plasmid and the inserted genetic daughter part. I found this experiment very interesting as it allowed me to understand the structure and function of plasmid DNA, as well as the principles and methods of restriction endonucleases and electrophoretic analysis.

 

 There are some shortcomings in my laboratory operations. For example, when I use a micropipette, I sometimes dispense the wrong amount, resulting in an uneven concentration of reagents. Also, when I perform electrophoresis analysis, I sometimes add DNA solution to the wrong tank or add too much or too little, which affects the effect of electrophoresis. I think these are all areas that I need to improve on and I need to practice more to improve my lab skills and accuracy.
